Section 02

The builder and the breed

To understand Bandido it helps to understand the breed she belongs to. The sport-fisher, or sport-fishing yacht, is one of the oldest distinct lineages in pleasure boating, and it grew up around a very specific problem. Game fish live offshore, often a long way offshore, and they hold in deep, sometimes lumpy water. To reach them in a single day and still have the comfort and the deck space to fight a big fish, you need a particular kind of boat: fast enough to cover ground, sea-kindly enough to make the run in comfort, and laid out around an open working cockpit rather than a sun-pad.

The classic answer evolved on the sport-fishing coasts of the world, where charter skippers needed boats that could run out to the canyons and seamounts at first light, fish hard all day and be home by evening. That heritage shaped every detail of the type: the raised bow for punching through a head sea, the broad transom and low freeboard aft for working a fish boatside, the flying bridge or raised helm so the skipper can read the water and watch the spread of lines, and the outriggers that fan the trolled baits wide of the wake. A true sport-fisher is a tool, and like all good tools it wears its function openly.

Bandido sits squarely in that tradition. She is a builder's sport-fishing yacht rather than a converted cruiser, which is the distinction that matters when the wind gets up and the fish are two hours out. The hull is a planing design driven by twin diesels, so she gets up and goes, and settles into an efficient cruise that makes distant marks a realistic proposition even on a half-day ticket. The layout keeps the working area clear and the freeboard sensible, so the mate can do his job and a guest can fight a fish without climbing over the furniture. None of this is glamorous on paper. All of it is the difference between a boat that looks like a fishing boat and a boat that fishes.

Section 03

What the class is known for

If you have only ever been on a leisure cruiser, the first thing you notice about a sport-fishing yacht is how purposeful she feels. There is a logic to where everything sits, and that logic is the day's fishing.

The defining features of the class are worth spelling out, because they explain why Bandido is built the way she is:

  • The cockpit is the heart of the boat. On a cruiser the action is up top on the sun-pad. On a sport-fisher it is down aft in the open cockpit, where the rods are worked, the fish is landed and the crew operate. Space, footing and a clear arc to swing a rod all matter more here than upholstery.
  • Outriggers spread the spread. Those long poles that fold up against the superstructure are not decoration. Lowered, they carry trolled lines out wide of the boat so several baits can be worked at once without tangling, and so the pattern looks natural to a fish following the wake.
  • Rod holders everywhere. A proper sport-fisher carries holders in the gunwales, around the cockpit and often in a rocket launcher behind the helm, so a full trolling spread can be set and tended quickly.
  • A livewell keeps bait fresh. Live or freshly rigged bait out-fishes everything, and a circulating well aboard keeps it that way through the day.
  • Speed and range are designed in. Twin diesels and a planing hull are not about thrills, they are about reaching grounds that a slower boat simply cannot fish and still get home.

Bandido carries this kit as standard. The outriggers, the spread of rods and the livewell are part of the boat, not extras you have to ask for. That is the practical meaning of the phrase ready to fish on arrival: you are not waiting for a crew to bolt on tackle, you are stepping onto a boat that is already a fishing platform.

Section 04

On board: layout and fishing rig

Bandido is an 11.5 metre boat, which is a well-judged size for this kind of work. She is big enough to take a sea comfortably and to carry eight guests with room to move, yet compact enough to be nimble around a hooked fish and economical on the run out. That balance is one of the reasons she earns her place in the fleet rather than simply being the biggest boat on offer.

The working layout follows the sport-fishing template. The open aft cockpit is the centre of operations, kept clear so the mate can set and clear lines and so guests have safe footing when a rod loads up. The helm is positioned to give the skipper a commanding view of the water and the trolled spread, which is how a good captain reads where the fish are likely to be and reacts the instant a line goes off. The outriggers fold neatly clear of the deck on the run and drop into place once the boat slows to trolling speed.

The headline equipment, all of it carried as standard, breaks down like this:

  • Outriggers to carry a wide, natural trolling spread
  • A full complement of rods matched to the kind of fishing this coast offers
  • A livewell to keep bait fresh and lively through the day
  • Twin diesels and a planing hull for fast, efficient runs to the offshore marks

What you will not find on Bandido is the spread of inflatable water toys that come with the leisure boats in the fleet, and that is by design. This is a fishing boat. The trade-off is a deck set up to do one thing extremely well rather than a dozen things adequately. For the guests this boat is aimed at, that is exactly the right trade.

Section 05

A day aboard, from Puerto Banus

The best way to understand Bandido is to walk through a day on her. Offshore fishing rewards an early start, so a full-day charter typically begins not long after first light, while the sea is at its calmest and the bite is often at its most reliable. You meet your skipper and mate at the berth in Puerto Banus, the marina does its early-morning bustle around you, and within a few minutes you are easing out past the breakwater with the town still half-asleep behind you.

Clearing the harbour, the skipper pushes the throttles forward and Bandido lifts onto the plane. The run offshore is part of the pleasure of the day. La Concha mountain stands over Marbella behind you, the coast slides away, and the water changes colour as the bottom drops off. Somewhere out here are the marks the skipper has in mind, and local knowledge is doing real work now, because the difference between an empty patch of sea and a productive one can be a few hundred metres of depth and structure.

Arriving on the grounds, the boat slows and the cockpit comes alive. The mate drops the outriggers, sets the rods and lays out a trolling spread, and the livewell earns its keep. Then comes the part everyone is there for: the wait, the watching of the lines, and the moment a reel screams off and the cockpit snaps to attention. The mate clears the other lines, a guest takes the rod, the skipper works the boat, and for a few minutes nothing else in the world matters. No fishing experience is required to be the one in the chair. That is what the crew are for.

Between runs there is time to take in where you are. Cold drinks come out of the cooler, there are light snacks aboard, and the conversation turns, as it always does on a fishing boat, to the one that got away and the one still to come. As the day eases off, the skipper turns Bandido back towards Puerto Banus, the marina lifting out of the haze ahead, and you come in with the satisfied tiredness that only a day on the open water gives you. A half-day charter follows the same rhythm in a shorter compass, with the skipper choosing nearer marks so the fishing time is not eaten up by the run.

Section 06

Cruising and fishing grounds

Puerto Banus is one of the best-placed departure points on the whole Costa del Sol, and that geography is a large part of what Bandido offers. From here the coast and the deeper water are both within easy reach, and the skipper can shape the day around conditions and the kind of fishing you are after.

The grounds break down roughly like this:

  • The inshore stretch along Marbella, the Golden Mile and round towards Cabopino to the east is the gentler water, useful for a shorter session, for younger or first-time anglers, and for days when the wind keeps the offshore marks out of reach. It is also the prettiest run, hugging the famous coastline with the mountains behind.
  • West towards Estepona opens up more coast and more options, with the skipper able to work the water along the way as conditions suggest.
  • The offshore grounds are where a sport-fishing yacht like Bandido truly comes into her own. With twin diesels and the range to push well off the beach, she can reach the deeper marks and the productive structure that the inshore boats never see. This is the deep-sea fishing the boat was built for, and it is the reason to choose her over a general charter boat.
  • The far western horizon runs down towards Gibraltar and the mouth of the Strait, some of the most famous water in the western Mediterranean, where the Atlantic and the Mediterranean meet and the marine life is correspondingly rich. Longer runs in this direction are a serious undertaking and are shaped entirely around sea state, season and what is feeding, but they show the kind of ambition the boat is capable of.

Where exactly you go on the day is the skipper's call, made on the morning with the latest conditions in hand. That is the advantage of chartering a crewed boat with local knowledge rather than simply hiring a hull: the people aboard know this water, and they will spend your charter where the fishing is best, not where the brochure says it should be.

Section 09

Seasonality and booking practicalities

The Costa del Sol has one of the kindest climates in Europe, and the fishing season here is correspondingly long. The high summer months of July and August bring the warmest seas, the calmest mornings and, inevitably, the heaviest demand, so dates in that window book out earliest and are worth confirming well ahead. The shoulder seasons of late spring and early autumn are a quiet secret: warm enough to be a pleasure, often calmer and less crowded, and frequently excellent fishing.

A few practicalities make for a better day:

  • Start early. Offshore fishing rewards the first light of the morning, when the sea tends to be flattest and the bite most reliable. The crew will suggest a departure time, and on a fishing charter it pays to take their advice.
  • Watch the wind, not the calendar. How far offshore Bandido can comfortably range on any given day is governed by sea state. The skipper makes that call on the morning, and a sensible plan flexes with the conditions rather than forcing a long run into a lumpy sea.
  • Come as you are. No fishing experience is required and no licence is needed on a crewed charter, because the boat and its skipper carry the necessary permissions. The crew handle the navigation, the bait and the tactics. You bring sun protection, soft-soled shoes and an appetite for the day.
  • Book the whole boat. A charter is exclusive to your group, up to eight guests, so the day is yours to shape with the crew.

Because Bandido is a single boat with a focused following, her calendar in peak season fills faster than a general day-cruiser's. If you have a date in mind, particularly in summer or over a holiday weekend, it is well worth getting the quote and the booking confirmed early rather than leaving it to chance.
