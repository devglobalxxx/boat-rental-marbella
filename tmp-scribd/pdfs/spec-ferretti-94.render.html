1. Overview: The Flagship of the Marbella Fleet

The Ferretti 94 is the superyacht-class option in our Marbella fleet, a 29 metre flagship from Italy's Ferretti shipyard built for the most demanding charters on the Costa del Sol. Most boats that work this coast trade on the simple pleasures of sunshine, a swim stop and a cold drink at anchor. The Ferretti 94 offers all of that and then adds the things that separate a genuine superyacht from a large day boat: scale, privacy, range and a full professional crew who run the day so that guests never have to think about it.

She measures 29 metres on the waterline, which places her at the very top of what is available for private charter from Puerto Banús. For a day charter she is rated for twelve guests, the same headcount permitted on commercial charters across much of our larger fleet, but the experience at that number is entirely different. Where a 12 metre yacht carrying nine or ten feels comfortably full, the Ferretti 94 carrying twelve feels unhurried, with whole decks left open for guests to spread out, find shade, find sun, or simply find a quiet corner away from the group.

Below decks she holds four ensuite cabins and sleeps eight guests overnight, which makes her as much a floating residence as a day yacht. She is crewed by a captain, mate, chef and stewardess, a full hospitality team rather than a single skipper, and she carries a sun deck, a beach club at water level, and a dedicated water toys garage. That combination is what allows her to move seamlessly from a quiet morning at anchor to an afternoon of activity off the swim platform without anyone reaching for a paddle or pumping up a toy by hand.

She is also the most capable cruiser we offer. Beyond the classic Marbella day routes, the Ferretti 94 is comfortable on a run down to Gibraltar and across to the Strait, and she is built for full weekly charters. For guests who want one yacht that can do a sociable afternoon along the Golden Mile, a long lunch at anchor off Puerto Banús, and then a multi-day cruise west towards the Atlantic, she is the natural choice. She stands out in our fleet not because she is the fastest or the flashiest, but because she is the most complete.

2. The Builder and the Marque: Ferretti of Forlì

To understand why a Ferretti carries the weight it does on a charter dock, it helps to know where the marque comes from. Ferretti was founded in the late 1960s by the brothers Alessandro and Norberto Ferretti, who began not as boatbuilders but selling and servicing cars and boats in the Emilia-Romagna region of northern Italy. The yard's move into building its own motor yachts in the 1970s coincided with the great post-war expansion of Mediterranean leisure boating, and Ferretti grew with it.

By the 1980s and 1990s the company had established itself as one of the defining names in the Italian flybridge motor yacht, the very category this vessel belongs to. Ferretti's reputation was built on a particular formula: a planing hull that delivered real performance, married to interiors and deck layouts that felt more like a designer apartment than a boat. The brand became a byword for a certain kind of confident, sociable, sun-soaked Italian luxury, the sort of yacht that looks entirely at home moored stern-to in Porto Cervo, Saint-Tropez or, indeed, Puerto Banús.

Over the following decades Ferretti expanded into the Ferretti Group, an umbrella that brought together some of the most respected names in yachting, including Riva, Pershing, Itama, CRN and the Custom Line and Wally brands. That breadth matters because it means the engineering, design and after-sales knowledge behind a Ferretti hull is drawn from one of the deepest benches in the industry. A Ferretti is not a one-off; it is the product of a yard that has built thousands of yachts and refined the same essential ideas across generations.

The marque is also known for collaborating with leading naval architects and designers, and for a house style that prioritises liveability. Ferretti yachts are designed to be used, by families and by groups, with wide side decks, generous flybridges and saloons that open up to the sea. That philosophy is exactly what makes a Ferretti such a good charter yacht. A boat conceived for private owners to enjoy with friends translates naturally into a boat that charter guests enjoy too. When you step aboard the Ferretti 94, you are stepping onto a yacht from a yard whose entire history has been about making time on the water feel effortless.

3. The Model and the Design: What the 94 Class Is Known For

Within the Ferretti range, a yacht in the 90-foot bracket sits firmly in flagship territory for the brand's flybridge line. At 29 metres, this is a yacht conceived as the largest expression of the classic Ferretti motor yacht before stepping up into fully custom superyacht territory. What that means in practice is a vessel that combines the brand's signature qualities at the most generous scale available in series production.

The defining feature of the class is the flybridge. On a yacht of this size the flybridge is not a token upper helm but a full second living space, typically running a large part of the length of the superstructure, with a second control station, sunbathing areas, shaded seating and very often a bar and dining setup. It is the social heart of the yacht under way, the place from which guests watch the coast slide past with the wind in their hair and the whole panorama of the bay open around them. The profile of this vessel, with its tall, glassed superstructure and the sweeping line of the flybridge, is instantly recognisable as a yacht of this generation.

The second hallmark is volume. Yachts of this class are designed with a wide beam carried well forward, which creates enormous interior volume for the length. That is why a 29 metre Ferretti can offer four full ensuite cabins, a main-deck saloon and dining area, a separate galley and crew quarters, without anything feeling cramped. The naval architecture prioritises a soft, comfortable ride and a stable platform at anchor, both of which matter enormously on a charter where guests may include children, older relatives and people who do not consider themselves natural sailors.

The third hallmark is the flow between inside and out. Ferretti's designers have long favoured large glazing, low-profile bulwarks and an easy transition from the saloon out to the aft cockpit and down to the water. On this class that culminates in a proper aft platform and beach club arrangement, so the sea is always within easy reach. The result is a yacht that feels open and connected to its surroundings rather than sealed off from them, which is precisely what you want for a day spent anchored in a Mediterranean bay.

4. On Board: Layout, Saloon, Cabins and Water Toys

The Ferretti 94 is arranged across several distinct zones, each with its own purpose, which is what allows a group of twelve to share the yacht comfortably without ever feeling on top of one another. The accommodation, drawn directly from this vessel's own specification, is built around four ensuite cabins that sleep eight guests overnight, each with its own private bathroom. For a day charter those cabins double as private changing rooms, somewhere to leave bags and towels, and a cool, quiet retreat out of the midday sun.

The main-deck saloon is the air-conditioned core of the yacht, a lounge and dining space framed by the large windows that are a Ferretti signature. This is where the group gathers when the breeze gets up, where lunch is served in comfort if the weather turns, and where the evening drifts on after the sun has dropped. Ahead of it sits the galley, the chef's domain, which on a yacht of this class is a serious working kitchen rather than a token pantry, capable of producing proper meals for a full complement of guests.

Moving outside, the aft cockpit is the natural social space at anchor, shaded and close to the water, with the saloon doors folding back so inside and out become one. Up top, the sun deck on the flybridge is the yacht's grandstand, the place to sunbathe, take in the view and watch the coast go by under way. At the stern, a beach club at water level transforms the back of the yacht into a swimming and lounging platform, the heart of the day for most charter groups, where guests step straight from the deck into the Mediterranean.

What sets the Ferretti 94 apart from smaller boats in the fleet is the dedicated water toys garage. Rather than lashing toys to the deck, a yacht of this size stows them in a purpose-built compartment and launches them off the swim platform when guests want them. That makes the activity side of the day effortless: the crew handles the launching and recovery, and guests simply enjoy the water. Taken together, the layout reads as a sequence of choices, sun or shade, in or out, social or private, that the yacht offers all day long, which is the real luxury of chartering at this scale.

5. A Day Aboard: A Charter from Puerto Banús

To picture what a charter on the Ferretti 94 is actually like, it helps to walk through a realistic day. Boarding is at Puerto Banús, Marbella's flagship marina, where the yacht waits stern-to among the rest of the harbour's famous fleet. Guests are welcomed aboard by the stewardess, shown around, and settled in the aft cockpit with a first cold drink from the included selection while the captain and mate make ready and the chef finalises the galley.

The yacht slips her lines and idles out through the harbour mouth, past the breakwater and the line of cafes and boutiques, and within a few minutes the open Mediterranean stretches ahead with the dramatic backdrop of La Concha, the mountain that towers over Marbella, behind. From the flybridge the whole coastline opens up. For the first leg the captain might run gently east or west along the shore, giving guests a sense of the famous Golden Mile from the water before settling the yacht into the rhythm of the day.

By late morning the yacht finds a swim stop, dropping anchor in clear water off one of the beaches or coves along the coast. This is when the yacht comes into her own. The crew launches the toys from the garage off the swim platform, the beach club comes alive, and guests swim, lounge and drift between the water and the shade of the cockpit. The chef serves lunch on board, and because the kitchen is a proper galley with a dedicated cook, this is a real meal eaten in comfort rather than a packed snack.

The afternoon unfolds at its own pace. Some guests stay in the water, some retreat to a cabin for a nap out of the heat, some take the flybridge for the sun and the view. The captain may move the yacht to a second anchorage to follow the light or the shelter, or run a stretch of coast at speed for the pleasure of the cruise. As the day cools, the yacht turns for home, and the run back into Puerto Banús in the softening late-afternoon light, the marina and the mountains glowing, is one of the quiet highlights of any Marbella charter. The crew handles every line; guests simply arrive back at the dock relaxed.

6. Itineraries and Cruising Grounds

Departing from Puerto Banús places the Ferretti 94 at the centre of one of the most cruisable stretches of the Spanish coast, with options that scale from a gentle afternoon to a serious passage. The natural starting point for almost every charter is a swim stop along the Golden Mile, the celebrated band of coastline between Puerto Banús and Marbella town lined with beach clubs and villas. Anchoring off here gives guests the postcard view, the marina to one side, the town to the other, and the great grey face of La Concha rising behind, all within a few minutes of leaving the berth.

Heading east, the coast runs towards Cabopino, with its photogenic marina and the dune-backed beaches of Artola, a slightly longer run that rewards guests with quieter water and a sense of having properly travelled for the day. Further along lie the beaches towards Calahonda and beyond, classic territory for a relaxed full-day cruise with a long lunch at anchor in the middle.

Heading west, the coast opens towards Estepona, a handsome working town with a pretty old quarter and a marina of its own, and an easy and popular destination for a day cruise from Puerto Banús. Beyond Estepona the coast continues towards the dramatic silhouette of the Rock and the town of Gibraltar, marking the gateway to the Strait where the Mediterranean meets the Atlantic. A run to Gibraltar is a genuine sea passage rather than a coastal hop, and it is exactly the kind of longer itinerary the Ferretti 94 is built for, an area where her range, comfort and full crew turn a long day or an overnight leg into a pleasure rather than an ordeal.

For guests with more time, the yacht's overnight accommodation and weekly-charter capability open up multi-day cruising along the whole Costa del Sol and into the Strait, with the option of pushing on towards the Atlantic resorts or simply spending several days exploring the bays, anchorages and harbour towns at a leisurely pace. Few boats on this coast can offer that full range, from a two-hour sociable afternoon to a week-long cruise, on a single hull.

9. Seasonality and Booking Practicalities

The Costa del Sol earns its name. Marbella enjoys one of the mildest climates in Europe, and the charter season runs long, but there are sensible windows worth understanding when planning a day on the Ferretti 94.

The core season runs roughly from May to October, with the warmest seas and the most settled weather through high summer in July and August. These are the months when a full day at anchor with the beach club open is at its best, when the water is warmest for swimming and the chance of a wash-out is at its lowest. They are also the busiest months, which means the flagship is in demand and the best dates go early. June and September are often the connoisseur's choice: the sea is warm, the days are long, the light is beautiful, and the coast is a little less crowded than at the absolute peak.

The shoulder months of April and October can deliver glorious charter days, particularly for cruising and sightseeing, though guests should expect cooler water and the occasional unsettled spell. Even in the winter, calm bright days are common on this coast, and the Ferretti 94's enclosed, air-conditioned saloon means a charter is never wholly weather-dependent in the way an open boat would be.

On practicalities, the single most important step is to book early, especially for peak summer weekends and for any longer or overnight itinerary, because there is only one flagship of this class and her best dates are spoken for well in advance. The team will discuss the day's weather window with you, since the captain always has the final say on conditions for the safety and comfort of guests. It is also worth raising any specifics at the quoting stage, such as dietary requirements for the chef, the age range of the group, whether children are aboard, and any special occasion the crew should know about, so the day can be shaped around you from the outset.
