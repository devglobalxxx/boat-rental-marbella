Section 02

The Builder: Overmarine and the Mangusta Marque

The Mangusta name belongs to Overmarine, an Italian shipyard founded by the Balducci family that has built large, fast, open-style yachts in Italy for decades. Mangusta is not a single model so much as a marque, a whole line of yachts that the yard launched to define a particular idea: the maxi open. The concept paired the long, low, aggressive profile of a sport boat with the volume and luxury of a much larger motor yacht, and it found an audience almost immediately among owners who wanted speed and style in equal measure.

Overmarine built its reputation on a few things that the grey hull in our fleet shares. The first is the open superstructure, a flowing deck-house that keeps the sheer line low and unbroken rather than stacking a tall flybridge on top. The second is a serious turn of speed, historically delivered through water-jet propulsion that let these yachts cruise far faster than the displacement and semi-displacement boats they sat alongside in the marina. The third is Italian fit-out, the leather, the lacquer and the joinery that you expect from a yard operating at this level.

Over the years the Mangusta line has spanned a wide range of sizes, from yachts in the 70 and 80 foot bracket up to genuine 50 metre-plus superyachts, and the marque became a familiar sight in the glamour ports of the Mediterranean, from the Côte d'Azur to the Balearics. A Mangusta at the dock is instantly readable, and that recognition is part of what you are chartering. When the grey hull idles out of Puerto Banús, people on the quay know exactly what they are looking at.

Section 03

The Model and the Design

The 80 foot Mangusta sits in the sweet spot of the range. It is large enough to deliver the full open-yacht experience, with a wide saloon, proper cabins and a generous aft deck, yet compact enough to remain agile and manageable for day work along a single coastline. The class is known for a low, planted stance on the water, a long foredeck that lends itself to a full-beam sun pad, and an aft cockpit that flows down to a broad swim platform at the waterline.

The defining feature of the type is the way it blurs the line between sport boat and motor yacht. From the helm it feels like a much smaller, sportier vessel. From the deck it feels like a superyacht. That duality is exactly what makes it such a good charter platform. Guests get the wow of stepping aboard something genuinely large, while the boat still has the manners to nip from anchorage to anchorage without it feeling like a slow procession.

The open layout also means the social spaces are connected. On a tall flybridge yacht the group tends to fragment between decks. On an open Mangusta the saloon, the cockpit and the foredeck sun pad all sit close together, so a party of twelve stays together rather than splitting up. For a celebration or a family day, that connectedness matters more than most people expect when they first step aboard.

Section 04

Speed, Sea-Keeping and the Feel Underway

One of the quiet pleasures of an open Mangusta is how it behaves underway. The whole point of the maxi-open concept was to give a yacht of this volume the manners of a sport boat, and that character carries through to the way the grey hull crosses the water. Where a tall, heavy flybridge cruiser tends to wallow and lumber, the long, low Mangusta hull settles onto its lines and tracks cleanly, so the run between anchorages feels purposeful rather than ponderous.

That matters more than it sounds for a day on the Costa del Sol. The local coast can pick up a short, choppy sea in the afternoon when the breeze fills in, and a hull with a fine entry and a planing attitude handles that far better than a slab-sided boat. Guests feel it as a smoother, drier ride, and the captain feels it as a yacht that responds rather than fights. The low centre of gravity of the open layout also keeps the motion gentle, which is part of why this type suits a mixed group that includes people who are not natural sailors.

At anchor, the same hull form pays off again. The broad beam carried well aft gives the boat a stable, planted feel when she is sitting still and loaded with guests moving between the swim platform, the cockpit and the foredeck. Nobody is lurching about as people climb in and out of the water. It is the kind of detail you never think about when it works, and the whole reason a flagship-scale yacht feels so relaxed to spend a long day on.

  • Full-beam sun pad: the long foredeck carries a generous sun pad that runs the width of the boat, the prime spot for sunbathing at anchor.
  • Air-conditioned saloon: step inside to leather seating and a cool, calm interior for the hottest part of the day or a quieter moment out of the sun.
  • Three cabins below: the grey hull has three cabins below deck, the kind of accommodation that makes longer and overnight charters possible when quoted accordingly.

The flow from water to cockpit to foredeck is what makes a day on this boat feel effortless. Nobody has to negotiate steep companionways to get from a swim to a sunbathe, and the crew can keep drinks moving without anyone having to leave their spot.

Section 06

A Day Aboard from Puerto Banús

Here is how a typical day charter unfolds. You arrive at the berth in Puerto Banús a little before your departure time, where the captain and deckhand meet you at the passerelle for a short welcome and safety briefing. Boarding is straightforward from the pontoon, bags go below, and within a few minutes the lines are slipped and the grey hull eases out past the breakwater.

The first leg is usually a gentle cruise along the coast while the group settles in, finds their spots on the sun pad and in the cockpit, and the first drinks come out. Marbella opens up to port, with the unmistakable bulk of La Concha mountain rising behind the town and the villas of the Golden Mile lining the shore.

Mid-morning to early afternoon is anchor time. The captain finds a calm spot in the lee of the coast, the swim ladder goes down, and the Sea-Doo comes off the platform. This is the centrepiece of the day, taking turns on the jet ski, swimming off the back of the boat, and laying out lunch in the cockpit. There is no rush. A shorter charter still gives you a proper swim stop, while a longer booking lets you move between two or three anchorages and stretch the afternoon out.

As the day winds down, the boat cruises back toward Puerto Banús, often timed so the light is at its best on the run home. You step ashore back at the marina with the kind of glow that only a full day on the water gives you. The crew handle everything in between, so the only decisions you make all day are which beach to anchor off and who gets the jet ski next.

Section 07

Itineraries and Cruising Grounds

From Puerto Banús the coastline opens up in both directions, and the grey Mangusta has the legs to reach the best of it comfortably within a day charter. The classic itineraries build naturally around the length of your booking.

  • The Golden Mile swim stop: the short hop east toward Marbella, anchoring off the Golden Mile where the water is calm and the backdrop of La Concha and the beachfront villas is at its most striking. Ideal for a shorter charter focused on swimming and the jet ski.
  • Cabopino: a longer run east past Marbella toward the dunes and pine-backed beaches around Cabopino, one of the prettier stretches of natural coast on this part of the Costa del Sol. A good half-day target.
  • Estepona: heading west, the cruise toward Estepona trades the glamour of Banús for a quieter, more relaxed shoreline, the kind of run that suits a full-day charter with a long lunch at anchor.
  • Longer runs toward Gibraltar: with more hours on the clock, the coast continues west toward the Strait, with the Rock of Gibraltar and the open water of the Strait of Gibraltar within reach for the most ambitious full-day plans.

Because the open Mangusta cruises with genuine pace, distances that would feel like a slog on a slower boat become an easy part of the day. The captain will tailor the route on the day to the weather and the sea state, always aiming for the calmest, prettiest water for your swim stops.

Section 11

Seasonality and Booking Practicalities

The Marbella charter season runs long, but the rhythm of the year is worth understanding when you plan. The high summer months, from roughly June through September, bring the warmest water, the longest days and the liveliest scene in Puerto Banús, and they are also the busiest, so the flagship boats book up well ahead for weekends and peak dates.

The shoulder months on either side, late spring and early autumn, are quietly some of the best times to take a 24 metre yacht out. The sea is settled, the light is beautiful, the coast is less crowded, and availability is easier to secure. A warm day in May or October on the grey Mangusta, with the jet ski off the back and a long lunch at anchor, is hard to beat.

A few practicalities make the day run smoothly. Arrive at the Puerto Banús berth a little before your departure time so the briefing does not eat into your water hours. Bring swimwear, towels, plenty of sunscreen, sunglasses and a layer for the cooler run home, and wear soft-soled shoes or go barefoot on deck. Drinks and light snacks are already aboard, and for longer charters you are welcome to bring your own food.
