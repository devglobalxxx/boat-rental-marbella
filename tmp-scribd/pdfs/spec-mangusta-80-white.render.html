Section 02

The Builder and the Marque: Overmarine and Mangusta

Mangusta is the yacht line of Overmarine, an Italian shipyard founded in the mid-1980s and based in Viareggio, the Tuscan town that has long been one of the spiritual homes of Italian yacht building. The name Mangusta, Italian for mongoose, was chosen deliberately: the mongoose is small relative to what it takes on, quick and fearless, and that is exactly the character Overmarine set out to build into its boats. From the start the brief was speed and style in equal measure, wrapped in a hull that could cross open water without drama and still look like nothing else in the marina.

Overmarine effectively created and then defined the modern open sport-yacht category. The first Mangusta 80 arrived at the end of the 1980s and was, at the time, a statement about what a fast luxury yacht could be: a long, sleek, predominantly flush deck profile, a vast social cockpit and sun-pad area up top, and the accommodation tucked away below rather than stacked into a tall superstructure. That low, horizontal silhouette became the brand's signature, copied across the industry but rarely matched. Over the decades the yard scaled the concept up dramatically, building open yachts well beyond 50 metres and adding the larger Oceano and GranSport ranges, yet the original recipe still defines what people picture when they hear the name Mangusta.

A few things have always travelled with the marque. One is engineering for speed: Mangustas have traditionally favoured surface-drive and waterjet propulsion, the systems that let a big hull reach genuinely high speeds rather than the sedate cruising pace of a conventional displacement yacht. Another is Italian craftsmanship below deck, with interiors finished to the standard you would expect from a Viareggio yard. And the third is that unmistakable design language, instantly legible from a distance, which is precisely why a Mangusta turns heads in a harbour as busy and as image-conscious as Puerto Banús. Chartering one is not only about the space and the comfort; it is about stepping aboard a piece of yachting design history.

The Model and the Design: What the 80 Is Known For

The 80, at 24 metres, has long been one of the most important models in the Mangusta line, and for many people it is the yacht that comes to mind when they think of the marque. That balance is exactly what makes it such a strong charter boat on the Costa del Sol.

The defining feature is the open sport-yacht layout. The result is a yacht that lives outdoors, which is the whole point on the Costa del Sol, where the appeal is sun, swimming and the coastline rather than hiding indoors. The other half of the 80's reputation is performance. Mangusta built its name on yachts that look fast and genuinely are, and the 80 was conceived to move: this is a hull designed to cover ground at a real cruising pace, not to potter. In practical charter terms that means the coastline opens up to you. A run that would feel like a long haul on a slower boat, whether down towards Estepona or across the bay to a quieter anchorage, becomes a comfortable, scenic transfer. You spend more of your day where you actually want to be, swimming and relaxing, and less of it simply getting there.

Section 05

A Day Aboard from Puerto Banús

Picture a typical charter day. You arrive at Puerto Banús a little before your start time and walk down the pontoon to where the white Mangusta sits among the marina's most photographed yachts. The deckhand takes your bags, the captain welcomes you aboard, and within a few minutes the lines are off and you are easing out past the breakwater. The fridge is already stocked with water, soft drinks, beer, white wine and cava, and there are light snacks to hand, so the day begins the moment you sit down in the cockpit.

Clear of the harbour, the captain opens her up and Marbella reveals itself from the water: the long sweep of beach, the white town climbing the hillside, and behind it all the dramatic limestone bulk of La Concha mountain. The first stop is usually a swim, often just off the Golden Mile, where the yacht drops anchor in clear water within sight of the famous beach clubs. This is where the Mangusta comes into its own. The jet ski comes off the platform, the more energetic guests take turns blasting across the bay, and everyone else swims, floats and stretches out on the foredeck sun-pad with a drink in hand.

By the middle of the day the cockpit becomes the centre of gravity. Guests gather in the shade for a long, lazy lunch as the boat swings gently at anchor. Afterwards there is time for another swim, another turn on the jet ski, or simply an afternoon of doing very little in the sun. Depending on the length of your charter and the mood of the group, the captain might lift the anchor and run a little further along the coast to a second, quieter spot, or hold position and let the afternoon drift. As the light softens towards the end of the day, you cruise back into Puerto Banús with the satisfying sense of having had the whole of Marbella's coastline to yourselves. You step off relaxed, sun-warmed and already thinking about the next time.

Section 06

Itineraries and Cruising Grounds

Puerto Banús is one of the best charter bases on the Costa del Sol precisely because of what sits within easy reach of it. The Mangusta's pace and range mean you are rarely limited by how far you can sensibly go in a day; the bigger question is simply what kind of day you want. A few classic patterns work especially well from this berth.

The Golden Mile swim stop. The shortest hop and the most popular. Just east of Puerto Banús, the Golden Mile runs along Marbella's most exclusive stretch of coast, home to landmark beach clubs and grand seafront villas. Anchoring here puts you in clear, sheltered water with the town and La Concha as a backdrop, close enough to feel part of the scene and far enough to have your own patch of sea. It is the natural choice for a four-hour charter and the centrepiece of most longer days.

West towards Estepona. Head the other way and the coast opens out, quieter and more natural, towards Estepona. This is a lovely run for a longer charter when you want to leave the busiest water behind and find a calmer anchorage for a swim and a long lunch, with the dramatic backdrop of the mountains dropping to the sea.

East towards Cabopino. In the other direction lies Cabopino, a pretty marina set beside the protected dunes of Artola, one of the prettier and more low-key stretches of this coastline. It makes a rewarding destination for a day charter that wants a change of scene from the Marbella waterfront.

Longer runs. Because the Mangusta covers ground comfortably, the western horizon is genuinely open to you on a full day. Towards Gibraltar and Tarifa the landscape becomes wilder, with the Rock rising out of the strait and, on a clear day, the coast of North Africa across the water. These are best planned as a full-day or extended charter and are well worth discussing with the captain when you book. Whatever shape your day takes, your captain knows these waters intimately and will steer you to the best water for the conditions, the season and the size of your group.

Seasonality and Booking Practicalities

The charter season on the Costa del Sol is long and generous. The coast enjoys some of the most reliable sunshine in Europe, and the calm, warm waters off Marbella make for comfortable cruising across a wide stretch of the year. The peak runs through the summer, roughly June to September, when the sea is at its warmest and the demand for a yacht of this size is at its highest. Late spring and early autumn, namely May and September into October, are quieter on the water but still very pleasant, and often the most relaxed times to be out, with softer light and fewer boats at the popular anchorages.

Because the Mangusta 80 (White) is one of the largest boats available for day charter in Marbella, summer weekends in particular book out well ahead. If you have a fixed date in July or August, especially for a celebration, the sensible move is to enquire early and secure it rather than leave it to chance.
