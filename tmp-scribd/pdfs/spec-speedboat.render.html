The boat for the short, sharp version of the day

Not every day on the water needs to be an eight-hour expedition. Sometimes the right charter is the quick one: an hour or two of open throttle along the Marbella shoreline, the spray coming off the bow, the coast scrolling past faster than you can quite believe, and back to the marina in time for lunch. That is exactly what the Speedboat is for. She is a seven-metre coastal speedboat in the Boat Rental In Marbella fleet, set up to carry up to six guests, departing from Puerto Banus with a licensed skipper aboard, and built around speed and fun rather than long lunches at anchor.

She stands out in the fleet precisely because she is the antidote to everything the big yachts are about. Where the Mangusta and the Maiora are floating villas that move slowly and luxuriously, the Speedboat is light, nimble and quick. She is the boat you take when you want sensation over ceremony: when the point of the outing is the rush of cutting across the bay, the laughter of a group hanging on as the bow lifts, and the simple pleasure of going fast on a flat blue sea. There is a reason small fast boats have always been the most universally loved thing on any waterfront. They deliver the most fun per minute of anything that floats.

That makes her one of the most accessible boats on the dock. A full-day superyacht charter is a serious booking for a serious occasion. A speedboat hop is something you can do on a whim: a couple of hours carved out of a beach holiday, a birthday treat, a way to show visiting friends the coast from the water without committing the whole day to it. Section 02

The breed: small, fast and built for the coast

The Speedboat in this fleet is listed with a builder of Various, which is honest and worth explaining rather than dressing up. Unlike the named Italian and Spanish marques elsewhere in the line-up, the speedboat slot is filled by whichever well-kept fast coastal boat in the seven-metre class is available and on the water that season. That is the practical reality of running a fast day-boat: these are working hulls that earn their keep on short charters, and the operation keeps a sound, well-maintained example ready rather than tying the listing to a single named model. When you book, you are booking the experience and the size class, and the team confirm the exact boat on the day.

What matters far more than the badge is the breed, and the breed is one of the oldest and best-loved in pleasure boating. The fast open coastal boat, the runabout, the bowrider, the sportboat, whatever name a given era hangs on it, has been the entry point to life on the water for the better part of a century. The recipe barely changes because it does not need to: a light planing hull, a powerful engine, an open layout that puts everyone in the air and the sun, and just enough boat to do it safely. The whole design exists to convert horsepower into grins.

The genius of the type is its efficiency of pleasure. A seven-metre boat like this gets onto the plane quickly, skips across flat water at a genuinely exciting clip, and turns and responds in a way that nothing large ever can. It is shallow enough in draught to nose into coves and right up to a beach, light enough to feel alive under you, and small enough that the whole group is together in one open cockpit rather than spread across three decks. That intimacy is part of the appeal. On a speedboat everybody is in on the same moment.

Section 03

What the class is known for

If you have only ever been on a big slow cruiser, the first thing the Speedboat teaches you is how different fast feels. A superyacht moves through the sea. A speedboat moves over it. Up on the plane the hull rides on a fraction of its length, the ride goes light and skittish in the best way, and the same patch of coast that takes a motor yacht half an hour to amble along goes by in a few exhilarating minutes.

The defining features of the class are worth spelling out, because they explain why this boat is built and used the way she is:

  • The open cockpit is the whole boat. There is no saloon to retreat to and no flybridge to climb. Everyone sits together in the open air, in the sun and the breeze, which is exactly where you want to be on a fast boat. It makes the outing sociable and immediate.
  • Speed is the headline feature, not a footnote. A planing hull and a strong engine are the point. The boat exists to go fast and to make that fun feel safe and easy, with a skipper who knows how to read the swell and pick the smoothest line across the bay.
  • Shallow draught opens up the coast. A small light boat can nose into spots the big yachts cannot reach, getting you close to the cliffs, the coves and the beach for a swim off the side.
  • Water sports come naturally. The same power and agility that make her fun to ride make her a natural tow boat, so an optional inflatable ride or a turn behind the boat fits the format perfectly.
  • Short and flexible by design. The whole point is the short hop. You are not committing to a full day. An hour or two is a complete, satisfying outing on a boat like this.

The Speedboat carries this character as standard. She is a fast coastal boat with a skipper aboard, set up for short-hop charters with the option of a bit of water-sport fun mixed in. That last point is one of the most underrated things about the format: a small fast boat is the most flexible thing in the fleet, able to be a sightseeing run, a swim trip, a water-sports session or a sundowner cruise depending entirely on the mood of the group.

Section 05

A day aboard, from Puerto Banus

The best way to understand the Speedboat is to walk through an outing on her, because she is the opposite of an all-day commitment and the rhythm is part of the charm. You meet your skipper at the berth in Puerto Banus, that famous marina doing its glamorous bustle around you, the superyachts lined up along the quay and the cafes filling up. Within a few minutes of stepping aboard you are easing out past the breakwater, and the moment you clear the harbour the day really begins.

The skipper opens the throttle and the boat lifts onto the plane. This is the moment everyone is there for. The bow comes up, the hull settles into its fast ride, the wind takes your hair and the engine note steadies into that satisfying drone, and Marbella starts to roll past at a pace that makes the whole group grin. La Concha mountain stands over the town behind you, the Golden Mile and its villas slide by to one side, and the water flattens out into the deep blue that this coast is famous for. There is nothing quite like seeing Marbella from a fast boat. It reorders the place in your mind.

Somewhere along the run the skipper eases off and noses the boat into a quieter patch of water for a swim stop. The engine drops to a murmur, someone is first over the side, and for a while the fast boat becomes a swimming platform anchored off a stretch of coast you would never reach from the beach. Cold drinks come out of the cool box, there are light snacks aboard, and the group floats in the clear water with the boat bobbing beside them. If the mood is for it, this is also where the water sports come in: a turn on an inflatable behind the boat, a tow across the flat water, the kind of thing that turns a nice outing into the highlight of a holiday.

Then it is back up on the plane for the run home, the marina lifting out of the haze ahead, and you come in flushed and salty and happy with that particular satisfaction a fast boat gives you. The whole thing can be done in an hour or two, which is the point. It slots into a day rather than swallowing it, and it leaves you wanting to do it again. Longer outings simply give the skipper room to range further along the coast and to linger longer at the swim stop.

Section 06

Cruising grounds from Puerto Banus

Puerto Banus is one of the best-placed departure points on the whole Costa del Sol, and for a fast boat that geography is a gift. The most scenic stretches of coast are all within an easy run, the water is generally calm in the mornings, and a quick boat can take in a remarkable amount of coastline in a short outing. The skipper shapes the run around conditions, the length of your charter and what the group fancies.

The grounds break down roughly like this:

  • The Golden Mile runs east from Puerto Banus towards Marbella town, the famous stretch of villas, beach clubs and grand hotels seen from the water. It is the classic short run, and a fast boat covers it in minutes, with a swim stop off one of the quieter sections a natural pause. Seeing the Golden Mile from the sea, with La Concha mountain rising behind it, is one of the signature Marbella experiences.
  • Cabopino, further east, is a pretty marina backed by the dunes of Artola, one of the few undeveloped stretches of this coast. On a longer outing it makes a fine turning point, with calm water for swimming along the way.
  • West towards Estepona opens up a quieter, more natural stretch of coast, with coves and clear water and far fewer boats. It is a lovely direction for a group that wants the scenery and the swim more than the glamour.
  • The shoreline itself is the speedboat's playground. Her shallow draught and small size let her nose into spots the big yachts simply cannot reach, close to the cliffs and right up towards the beaches, which is exactly the kind of coastline a fast nimble boat is made to explore.

Where exactly you go on the day is the skipper's call, made on the morning with the latest conditions in hand. That is the advantage of chartering a crewed boat with local knowledge rather than simply hiring a hull. The skipper knows where the water will be flattest, where the prettiest swim spots are, and how to give a short outing the most scenery and the most fun. On a fast boat, distance is cheap, so even a short charter can take in a generous slice of this coast.

Section 09

Seasonality and the best time to go

The Costa del Sol has one of the kindest climates in Europe, and the season for a fast coastal boat is correspondingly long. The high summer months of July and August bring the warmest sea for swimming, the calmest mornings and, inevitably, the heaviest demand, so dates in that window book out earliest and are worth confirming ahead. The water is at its most inviting and the swim stops are at their best.

The shoulder seasons of late spring and early autumn are a quiet secret on this coast. May, June, September and into October are often warm, frequently calmer and far less crowded than the peak, and they make wonderful conditions for a fast outing. The sea is settled, the light is beautiful and the coast is quieter, so a swim stop feels like you have the place to yourselves. For a boat whose whole appeal is the ride and the swim, these months are arguably the best of the year.

A few practicalities make for a better outing:

  • Mornings are usually flattest. On this coast the sea tends to be at its calmest early, before the afternoon breeze gets up. A flat sea makes for the smoothest, fastest, most comfortable ride, so an earlier slot is often the best one. The skipper will advise.
  • Watch the wind, not the calendar. A small fast boat is most fun on settled water. If the wind is up, the skipper will keep the run sheltered and choose the smoothest line, and on the rare genuinely rough day will suggest moving the booking. A sensible plan flexes with the conditions.
  • Bring sun protection and a towel. The cockpit is open, the swim stop is part of the outing, and there is little shade on a boat built for speed. Sun cream, a hat and a towel make all the difference.
  • Book the whole boat. A charter is exclusive to your group, up to six guests, so the outing is yours to shape with the skipper.

Because the Speedboat is a single, much-loved boat doing short turns, her calendar in peak season can fill quickly, with several outings possible in a single good day. If you have a date and a slot in mind, particularly a summer morning or a holiday weekend, it is well worth getting the quote and the booking confirmed early rather than leaving it to chance.
