An Introduction to the K80

She is built by K Yachts, departs from Puerto Banús, and carries up to twelve guests for a day along the Costa del Sol. In a fleet that already runs to flagship motor yachts and superyachts, she occupies a deliberate place at the very top of the range, alongside our Mangusta 80, while bringing a markedly different character of her own.

What sets the K80 apart is not a single headline feature but the way her scale changes the experience of a day on the water. At twenty-four metres she has the volume to give a group of friends room to spread out, to set a long table for lunch, to sunbathe on a broad foredeck while others swim, and to retreat into air-conditioned shade when the afternoon sun is at its strongest. She has the presence of a private yacht rather than a hire boat, and she is run with the relaxed, all-inclusive approach that defines the rest of the fleet: a professional crew, drinks and snacks aboard, fuel and insurance handled, and a single tailored quote arranged before you step on.

She is, in short, a yacht for occasions that deserve something out of the ordinary. Multiple cabins below deck mean she can be discussed for overnight and longer arrangements as well as single days, which is unusual among the day boats working out of Puerto Banús. For a milestone birthday, a family gathering or a day of corporate hospitality, the K80 gives you a flagship-class platform without the commitment of ownership, and a crew who make the whole day effortless.

Section 02

The Builder and the Marque

The K80 is built by K Yachts, the name carried on her hull and the marque under which she sails in our fleet. A yacht of this size is rarely the product of a single hand. Vessels in the twenty-four metre class are the result of a long collaboration between a naval architect who shapes the hull and its behaviour at sea, an exterior designer who gives the yacht its profile and the lines you notice from the quay, and an interior team who turn raw volume into cabins, a saloon and the spaces where guests actually spend their day.

It helps to understand what that scale represents in yacht-building terms. A twenty-four metre motor yacht sits right on the threshold that the industry treats as the start of the superyacht category, the point at which a vessel stops being a large pleasure boat and becomes a small ship with the systems to match: generators, climate control throughout, fresh-water makers on longer passages, stabilisation to keep her steady at anchor, and the tankage to cruise for a full day without thinking about it. Building at this length is a different discipline from building a sports cruiser. The margins for error in weight, balance and finish are smaller, and the engineering that guests never see, the wiring, the plumbing, the sound insulation, is where a great deal of the work goes.

The Mediterranean, and the stretch of coast between Gibraltar and Marbella in particular, has long been one of the world's great proving grounds for motor yachts of this kind. The combination of reliable summer weather, deep natural anchorages and a clientele who expect the best has drawn the finest yards and designers to these waters for decades. A yacht that works here has to look the part against serious company and behave well in the short, choppy sea that the Levante and Poniente winds can throw up. The K80 was conceived for exactly this environment, and she carries herself accordingly.

Section 03

The Model and Her Design

The K80 belongs to the flagship motor-yacht class, the segment around the twenty-four metre mark that the charter market prizes above almost any other. There is a reason this size is so sought after. It is large enough to deliver genuine superyacht volume, with multiple decks, full standing headroom below and cabins worthy of the name, yet still compact enough to slip into a berth at Puerto Banús and to run an agile day charter along the coast rather than only long passages.

Yachts of this class are known for a particular set of qualities. The first is a flybridge or upper helm and sun area that lifts guests above the water, giving the long views of the coast and the open horizon that make a day at sea feel expansive. The second is a broad main deck and foredeck that turn into sun-lounging and dining space the moment the yacht drops anchor. The third is a low, sociable aft deck close to the water, the natural gathering point of any charter, where lunch is served, swimmers come and go from the bathing platform, and the afternoon slips away. A yacht in this segment is designed so that twelve people can be aboard and never feel crowded, with quiet corners for those who want them and open space for those who do not.

The other hallmark of the class is the balance between the two halves of a charter. Underway, a yacht like the K80 should hold a comfortable cruising pace and ride cleanly, so the run between anchorages is part of the pleasure rather than a means to an end. At rest, she should sit steady and quiet, a stable platform for swimming, eating and simply being still on the water. The design choices that achieve both at once, the hull form, the weight distribution, the layout of the decks, are precisely what define a good motor yacht of this length, and they are what the K80 is built around.

Section 05

A Day Aboard from Puerto Banús

The best way to understand the K80 is to walk through a typical day charter, the kind of full day that a group of friends or a family might book in high summer. The picture below is a realistic composite of how these days unfold from Puerto Banús. The exact timings are flexible and are agreed with the crew on the day to suit the weather and what you want from your time aboard.

You arrive at Puerto Banús in the late morning. The marina is an easy meeting point, with parking, cafés and the beach all within a few minutes' walk, and the K80 is one of the more commanding silhouettes on the quay. The captain and deckhand greet you, take any provisions you have brought, run through the safety basics, and you settle in with a cold drink as the lines come off. Within minutes the harbour mouth is behind you and the whole of the Bay of Marbella opens up ahead.

The crew ease the yacht eastward along the Golden Mile, the famous stretch of coast between Puerto Banús and Marbella town lined with villas, beach clubs and palm-fringed gardens. After a short run the engines come down to a murmur and the K80 settles at anchor in clear water off the coast. The deckhand sets out the water toys, the bathing platform comes alive, and the first long swim of the day begins. Some guests are in the sea straight away, others stretch out on the sun deck with a glass of cava, and the day finds its pace.

Around the middle of the day, lunch is served aboard. Light snacks and the full range of included drinks, water, soft drinks, beer, white wine and cava, keep the table going, and many groups bring or arrange catering for something more substantial. After lunch the captain might lift the anchor and run a little further along the coast to a second, quieter spot, so the afternoon swim happens somewhere new. As the sun begins to soften, the K80 turns for home, cruising slowly back along the Golden Mile with the coastline glowing gold and Puerto Banús drawing closer. You step back onto the quay relaxed, a little sun-kissed, and already talking about the next time.

Section 06

Itineraries and Cruising Grounds

Puerto Banús sits in the middle of one of the most rewarding cruising coasts in the western Mediterranean, with options in both directions and the whole of the Strait of Gibraltar within reach for a longer day. Because the K80 is quoted by arrangement, her routes are shaped around your group rather than a fixed timetable, and the crew choose anchorages on the day to match the wind and sea. These are the cruising grounds that open up from her home port.

  • The Golden Mile and a swim stop off Marbella. The shortest and most popular run, east from Puerto Banús along the villa-lined Golden Mile to anchor in clear water within sight of the coast. Ideal for a relaxed half day built around swimming and lunch rather than long passages.
  • Cabopino and the dunes. Further east lies the pretty marina of Cabopino, set beside the protected Artola dunes and some of the cleanest beaches on this coast. The water here is often a little quieter, making it a fine choice for a second anchorage on a full day.
  • Estepona to the west. Heading the other way, the coast towards Estepona is greener and less built up, with a string of bays that reward a longer cruise. A run west and back makes a satisfying full day with a swim stop at the turn.
  • Longer runs towards Gibraltar. On a calm day and a longer charter, the dramatic profile of Gibraltar and the mouth of the Strait come within range, with the Rock rising at the western end of the bay and the coast of Africa visible across the water. These are best discussed as full-day or extended itineraries.

Whichever direction you choose, the principle is the same. The K80 is at her best on a day built around one or two unhurried anchorages, with the cruising between them treated as part of the pleasure rather than a sprint from A to B. The crew will steer you towards the sheltered side of the coast depending on whether the day's wind is a Levante from the east or a Poniente from the west, so the swimming stops stay calm and comfortable.

Section 09

Seasonality and Booking Practicalities

The charter season on this coast runs broadly from spring into autumn, and the K80 rewards a little thought about when you sail. The high summer of July and August brings the warmest sea, the longest days and the liveliest atmosphere in Puerto Banús, and it is also when demand is at its peak. Popular summer weekends on a flagship yacht of this kind are reserved well in advance, so for July and August an early enquiry is sensible if you have a fixed date in mind.

The shoulder months either side, May, June and September into early October, are quietly the connoisseur's choice. The weather is still reliably warm, the sea has the day-long calm that makes for easy swimming, the coast is less crowded and availability is better. Many regular guests prefer these weeks precisely because the experience feels less rushed and the same yacht is easier to secure on the date you want.

A word on the weather itself. This stretch of coast is shaped by two prevailing winds, the Levante from the east and the Poniente from the west, and the captain plans the day around whichever is blowing, working the sheltered side of the coast so your swim stops stay calm. On the rare day when conditions are genuinely unsuitable, the crew will always put safety and your comfort first.
