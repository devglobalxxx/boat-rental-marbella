Section 01

Overview: the sport yacht of the Marbella fleet

Some boats are built to drift, and some are built to move. The Pershing 46 belongs firmly to the second group. She is a 14-metre Italian sport yacht with a top speed of more than 30 knots, a sleek dark interior, two cabins and room for ten guests, and she is one of the more characterful charters working out of Puerto Banús. Where a flybridge cruiser asks you to slow down and settle in, the Pershing asks a different question. It asks how much of the coast you would like to see, and how quickly you would like to get there.

That distinction matters on this stretch of the Costa del Sol. The waters off Marbella are wide and open, the Golden Mile and its swim stops are a short hop from the marina, and places that feel like a serious commitment in a slower boat (Estepona to the west, Cabopino to the east, the far headlands toward Gibraltar) come within easy reach when the hull underneath you is a genuine planing sport yacht. The Pershing 46 turns the geography of the coast from a constraint into a menu.

She is also, simply, a good-looking boat. The marque has spent decades refining a particular visual language of low, knife-edged profiles and dark, tailored interiors, and the 46 carries it well. For a group that wants their day on the water to look as sharp as it feels (a milestone birthday, a stag or hen celebration, a client they want to impress, a family that has graduated from the gentle pootle and now wants some thrill) she hits a specific and quite desirable note. This dossier walks through who builds her, what the class is known for, what a day aboard actually looks like, where she can take you from Puerto Banús, what is included, and how to book. Every hard figure here, from her length and her capacity to her cabin count, her top speed and what comes with the charter, is drawn from her listing. The colour around those figures is general context about the boat, the marque and the coast, and is flagged as such throughout.

Section 02

The builder and the marque: the Pershing name

To understand why the Pershing 46 looks and behaves the way she does, it helps to know a little about the name on her hull. Pershing is one of the best-known sport-yacht marques to come out of Italy, and the brand has built its entire identity around a single idea executed with unusual discipline: fast boats that look as fast standing still as they do at speed. Founded in the Adriatic boatbuilding heartland of Italy in the 1980s, the marque set out from the beginning to merge open-water performance with the kind of styling more often associated with sports cars than with motor yachts.

That ambition shaped a whole house style. Over the decades the yard became part of the wider Ferretti Group, one of the largest luxury-yacht builders in the world, which gave Pershing the engineering depth and the design resources of a major industrial shipyard while letting the marque keep its distinct, performance-first character. The result is a brand with a recognisable signature: long, low, wedge-shaped profiles, deeply raked windscreens, retractable hardtops on the larger models, and interiors finished in dark woods, smoked glass and brushed metal rather than the pale, beachy palette of a typical holiday cruiser.

Pershing yachts have long been a fixture of the glamorous Mediterranean ports, from Monaco and Saint-Tropez to Porto Cervo, and very much Puerto Banús, which has always had a soft spot for a fast Italian boat with attitude. The marque became shorthand for a certain kind of confident, design-led owner, and its boats have appeared often enough in film, advertising and the harbours of the rich and visible that the silhouette is genuinely iconic. None of that is a hard spec of this particular vessel, but it is the reason she carries the presence she does. When you charter a Pershing, you are chartering into a lineage that has spent forty years perfecting the marriage of speed and style, and you can feel that intent in every line of the boat.

Section 03

The model and the design: what this class is known for

Within the marque's range, a boat of this size sits in a sweet spot that owners and charterers have always prized. A 14-metre sport yacht is large enough to feel like a proper yacht, with cabins, a saloon, a galley and the deck space to host a real group, yet small and light enough to deliver the headline figure that defines this class: genuine speed. A top speed of more than 30 knots is not a number you reach on a heavy displacement cruiser. It is the product of a planing hull, a powerful engine installation and a relentless focus on keeping weight and drag down, and it changes the entire experience of being aboard.

What this class is known for, beyond the raw pace, is the way it carries that pace. A well-sorted sport yacht of this size feels planted and composed at speed rather than nervous, sitting confidently on its plane with the bow trimmed and the wake peeling away cleanly behind. The dark, sport-yacht interior is part of the same philosophy. Where a family cruiser maximises soft, pale, sun-drenched lounging space, the sport-yacht layout is tighter and more driver-focused, built around the helm and the open cockpit, with the accommodation below decks tuned for two cabins of comfortable, well-finished space rather than spread thin across many.

That is the trade the design makes, and it is a deliberate one. You give up some of the sprawling sunbathing acreage of a bigger flybridge boat, and in return you get a vessel that is quicker, sharper to look at, more involving to be aboard and far more capable of covering distance. For the right group it is exactly the right trade. The Pershing 46 is not trying to be the most spacious boat in the marina. She is trying to be the most exciting one, and within her class that is a target she is built precisely to hit.

Section 04

On board: layout, deck, cabins and the experience

Step aboard and the layout makes the boat's intentions obvious. The heart of the Pershing 46 is the open cockpit and helm, a low, wide social space wrapped around the driving position, where the group naturally gathers when the boat is on the move and where the action of the day plays out. This is where you feel the sport yacht for what it is: close to the water, close to the helm, and built for people who want to be part of the run rather than tucked away from it.

From her listing, the key features that define a day aboard are clear:

  • 14-metre Pershing sport yacht: a substantial boat with genuine presence, not a small day-runner.
  • Italian high-performance design: the marque's signature blend of speed and styling, inside and out.
  • 30-plus knot top speed: the headline that defines what this charter can do and where it can reach.
  • Ten guests, two cabins: comfortable for a full group by day, with two finished cabins below.
  • Dark Italian interior, sport-yacht layout: smoked, tailored and driver-focused rather than pale and beachy.

The two cabins below deck give the boat a dimension that a pure day-boat lacks. They provide somewhere private to change, to store bags and belongings out of the sun, to step out of the heat for a moment, and to keep the day's gear stowed and tidy. For most charters out of Puerto Banús the day is spent up top, at the helm, in the cockpit and on the bathing platform at the stern, in and out of the water at the swim stops, but the dark, well-finished accommodation below is there when you want it, and it is a meaningful part of what makes ten guests feel comfortable rather than crowded over a long day on the water.

The stern bathing platform is the social anchor of any swim stop. Drop off the plane, cut the engines, and the back of the boat becomes the place where the group gathers, easing in and out of the water, passing drinks up from the cooler, drying off in the sun. On a sport yacht like this the platform sits low and close to the surface, which makes getting in and out of the sea easy and turns every anchorage into a natural swimming spot.

Section 05

A day aboard: a charter from Puerto Banús, hour by hour

The best way to understand the Pershing 46 is to walk through a day. The following is a realistic shape for a charter from Puerto Banús; exact timings flex with the booking, the season and what the group wants, but it gives an honest sense of the rhythm.

Late morning, boarding at Puerto Banús. You meet your licensed skipper at the berth in Marbella's most famous marina. Bags go below into one of the two cabins, the cool box is already stocked with the included drinks, and the skipper runs through the simple safety briefing. Within a few minutes the lines are off and the boat is idling out past the breakwater, the superyachts and the harbourfront cafés sliding by on either side.

Midday, onto the plane. Clear of the harbour limit, the skipper opens her up, and this is the moment the boat earns her name. The bow lifts, she settles onto her plane, and 30-plus knots of Italian sport yacht turns the open water off Marbella into something exhilarating. The coastline scrolls past far faster than it would on a slower boat, and the first swim stop along the Golden Mile arrives in minutes rather than the best part of an hour.

Early afternoon, the first swim stop. The skipper finds a calm patch of water off the beaches, cuts the engines and drops the anchor. The change of pace is total: from the rush of the run to the stillness of a Mediterranean anchorage, the only sound the water against the hull. The bathing platform comes alive, the cold drinks come out, and the group spreads between the water and the deck. This is the heart of the day: the contrast between speed and stillness that a sport yacht does better than anything else.

Mid afternoon, a run along the coast. Back on the plane, you push further along the shore. Depending on the length of the charter and the conditions, that might mean a fast run east toward Cabopino, west toward Estepona, or a longer reach toward the dramatic headlands further down the coast. The point of a boat like this is that the distance is never the limiting factor. You can see a great deal of coast and still have time to stop.

Late afternoon, second stop and the run home. A second anchorage, another swim, the last of the snacks and a final drink as the light starts to soften and go golden over the water. Then the skipper points the bow back toward Puerto Banús and brings her home, the marina lights coming up as you ease back into the berth. A full day, a lot of coast covered, and the particular satisfaction of having done it aboard a boat that looks as good as the day felt.

Section 06

Itineraries and cruising grounds from Puerto Banús

Puerto Banús is one of the best charter bases on the whole Spanish coast, and a fast boat makes the most of it. Because the Pershing 46 covers ground so easily, the usual question of how far you can realistically get in a day has a generous answer. These are the natural cruising grounds from her home port. The destinations are real geography; the exact route on any given day is set by the skipper according to conditions, the length of the charter and what the group wants.

  • The Golden Mile swim stop. The strip of coast between Puerto Banús and Marbella town is the classic short hop: minutes away, sheltered, and lined with the beaches and beach clubs that made the area famous. It is the default first stop for a swim and the easiest grounds when time is short or the group simply wants to relax close to base.
  • Cabopino, to the east. Heading east past Marbella you reach the pretty marina and dune-backed beaches around Cabopino, a favourite anchorage with a different, quieter character to the glamour of Banús. On a fast boat it is an easy run rather than an expedition.
  • Estepona, to the west. West of Puerto Banús the coast opens out toward Estepona, with its long beaches and relaxed feel. It is a satisfying destination for a longer charter and a natural turning point for a full day out.
  • The longer runs. When the conditions are right and the charter is long enough, the speed of the boat genuinely opens up the wider coast, the dramatic headlands further west and the distant prospect of Gibraltar and the strait. These are reaches that a slower boat would treat as a serious undertaking; here they become a possibility you can actually choose on the day.

The honest framing is this: a sport yacht does not change the map, but it changes how much of the map you can use. The same number of hours buys you noticeably more coast, more swim stops and more variety than a slower boat would, which is exactly why a group with somewhere in mind, or a group that simply wants to keep moving, gravitates toward a boat like this.

Section 07

Who this boat suits

The Pershing 46 is not the right boat for every charter, and being clear about that is part of recommending her well. She is a sport yacht built around speed and style, with two cabins and capacity for ten guests, and she suits some groups and occasions far better than others.

She is an excellent fit for:

  • Groups who want some thrill. If the appeal of a day on the water is partly the rush of a fast boat opening up across the bay, this is the charter for you. The 30-plus knot top speed is the whole point, and it is wasted on anyone who only wants to drift.
  • Celebrations with a sense of occasion. Milestone birthdays, stag and hen parties, a group treating themselves: the boat's looks and pace give the day an unmistakable sense of event. She arrives and departs like she means it.
  • Style-conscious charters. For a group who care how the day looks as much as how it feels, a client to impress, photographs that will end up everywhere, the dark Italian design photographs as sharply as it presents in the marina.
  • Couples and small groups wanting range. With ten-guest capacity she handles a full group, but she is just as good for a smaller party who want to cover real distance and reach the quieter anchorages along the coast.

She is a less obvious choice for a charter built entirely around slow, sprawling sunbathing for a very large group, where a bigger, beamier flybridge cruiser with more flat deck acreage might suit better. But for the specific brief of speed, style and Italian swagger with ten aboard, she is one of the most characterful boats you can put your group on out of Puerto Banús.

Section 09

Seasonality and booking practicalities

The charter season on the Costa del Sol runs long, and the Marbella climate is one of the most reliable in Europe for time on the water. The broad shape of the year is worth understanding when you plan, and the following is general context for the coast rather than a fixed guarantee for any one date, since sea conditions are always set by the weather on the day.

High summer, roughly July and August, is the peak: the warmest seas, the longest days, the liveliest scene in Puerto Banús, and the busiest charter calendar. For a fast, characterful boat like this in peak weeks, booking well ahead is strongly advised, because the most sought-after dates and weekend slots go early. Late spring and early autumn, covering May, June, September and into October, are many people's favourite windows: warm water, plenty of sun, calmer marinas and a more relaxed feel, often with a little more flexibility on dates. The shoulder months at either end of that range can deliver beautiful days too, with the understanding that conditions are a touch more variable.

A few practical points worth knowing:

  • Book ahead for the boat you want. A specific, in-demand sport yacht is not infinitely available. The earlier you confirm, the more likely you are to get this exact boat on the date you want.
  • Conditions come first. A planing boat is at her best in settled water, and your skipper will always make the call that keeps the day safe and enjoyable, adjusting the route to the sea state. That judgement is part of what the licensed skipper is there for.
  • Tell them the shape of your day. The more the team knows up front, including group size, the date, how long you want to be out and whether you have a destination in mind, the more accurate the quote and the better the planning.
  • Everyday gear is all you need. Swimwear, a towel, sun cream, a layer for the run home. The skipper, the safety equipment, the drinks and the snacks are all already aboard.
